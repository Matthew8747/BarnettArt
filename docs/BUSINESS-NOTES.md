# Business notes — registering, tax & the web-design studio idea

> Practical, plain-English notes — **not** legal or accounting advice, but
> grounded in current UK guidance (sources at the bottom). Two topics: (1) does
> Anna need to register a business to sell art, and (2) is it worth the engineer
> setting up their own web-design studio for the CV/LinkedIn.

---

## 1. Does Anna need to register a business? — **Not yet**

In the UK you can sell things casually without registering anything **until your
trading income passes the £1,000 "trading allowance" in a tax year** (6 April →
5 April).

- **Under £1,000 of sales in the year:** nothing to do. No registration, no tax
  return for it. This likely covers the demo/early phase.
- **Over £1,000:** you must register as a **sole trader** with HMRC and file a
  Self Assessment return. The deadline is generous — register by **5 October
  after** the end of that tax year (e.g. cross it in 2026/27 → register by
  5 Oct 2027).

Important detail: the £1,000 is **gross income, not profit**. If you sell £1,200
of art but spent £500 on materials, you've still passed the £1,000 line and must
register (you'd then only pay tax on the profit).

**So, for now:** sell freely, but **keep a simple record** of every sale (date,
item, amount) and keep receipts for costs. If/when sales cross £1,000, registering
is free and takes minutes online. There's no need to register "just in case."

> **VAT** is a totally separate, much higher threshold (£90,000/yr) — irrelevant
> here. The Terms page assumes "not VAT-registered"; keep that unless it changes.

### Sole trader vs limited company
For a small art shop, **sole trader** is the right default: free, minimal
admin, and you only register once you cross £1,000. A limited company only starts
to make sense at higher, steady profits (some tax efficiency + liability
separation) and costs more to run. Don't bother with one for this.

## 2. The engineer's own web-design studio — **realistic, and worth it**

Setting up a small studio name to put on your CV/LinkedIn (e.g. *"designed and
built this client project under my own studio, for a fee"*) is a legitimate,
common move — **as long as it's true**: you did the work and actually invoiced
for it, even a small amount.

The mechanics are the same £1,000 rule, and it applies to **you separately** from
Anna (your trading income is yours; hers is hers — each person gets their own
£1,000 allowance):

- **Pick a studio name.** A trading name is fine — you don't register a name for
  a sole trader (only Ltd company names are registered at Companies House). Avoid
  another company's name/trademark.
- **Do the work and invoice it.** A simple invoice (your name/studio, the client,
  what you did, the amount, the date) makes it real and gives you the CV line
  honestly.
- **Register only when you cross £1,000** of your own freelance income in a tax
  year — same HMRC sole-trader registration as above, same 5 October deadline.
- **CV/LinkedIn framing:** "Founder / Freelance design engineer, *[Studio name]*
  — designed and built an e-commerce + portfolio platform for an independent
  artist (Next.js, Stripe, security-hardened, deployed on Vercel)." That's a
  strong, truthful entry whether or not you've formally registered yet.

**Is it worth it?** Yes — low effort, no cost until you're earning, and it turns
this project into a *client engagement* on paper rather than a hobby. The honest
bar is simply: real work, real (even small) invoice.

## 3. Quick "do I need to act?" checklist

| Situation | Action |
|---|---|
| Demo site up, no sales | Nothing. Keep records ready. |
| Selling, under £1,000/yr (either of you) | Keep a record of sales + costs. No registration. |
| Crossed £1,000/yr (art **or** web work) | Register that person as a sole trader with HMRC (free); file Self Assessment by the following 31 Jan. |
| Income around £90,000/yr | Look at VAT (not a concern here). |
| Want liability separation / higher profits | Consider a limited company (probably not needed). |
